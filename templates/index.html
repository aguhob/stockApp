About Elliott Wave

Waves in nature

Waves occur in nature, and usually in response to the energy of waves before it. The fibonacci sequence is an expression of this pattern: 0, 1 are added for the next number in the sequence, 1.
1 and 1 are 2, and so on. 0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5...

How waves behave

The wave diagrams above illustrate Elliott Wave's theory of how wave impulses behave. They start, back off slightly, then accelerate. Finally, they fade.

Then it starts over.

The pattern can go up OR down. And it is fractyl, meaning it's part of a larger "meta-" wave (going up OR down!) and also composed of small "underlying" waves.

A fibonacci analysis

Note the points labeled "1", "2", etc.

"Wave 1" is a wave that ends at "1." Note the proportion of this wave to "Wave 2", and to "Wave 3." (Note how long Wave 3 goes - this is the pattern we seek, upward OR downward.)


Elliott Wave says that whatever the wave pattern, one can zoom in or out of a wave, to adjust one's "scope" in order to discover an underlying or meta-fibonacci pattern. The tricky thing is determining the correct scope, to determine whether the pattern is heading up or down.
